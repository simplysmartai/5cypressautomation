# Reference: B2B Email Examples & Patterns
# 5 Cypress Automation — Knowledge Base

These examples and patterns represent effective B2B email approaches for tech and medical companies. Use these when writing email campaigns to model structure, tone, and technique — not to copy verbatim.

---

## Core Patterns That Work in B2B Email

### Pattern 1: The Problem-First Open
Start by naming the reader's pain before offering anything.

**Structure:**
> [Pain point in one sentence.]
> [Why it happens / why it's hard.]
> [Transition: "That's why we..."]

**Example opening:**
> Most [job title]s we talk to are still doing [painful manual thing] by hand.
> Not because they don't know there's a better way — but because switching feels risky.
> We built [product] specifically for teams who've been burned by that risk before.

---

### Pattern 2: The Single Insight Email
One useful idea. No pitch. Builds trust over time.

**Structure:**
> "Here's something we've noticed / learned / seen..."
> [The insight in 2-3 paragraphs]
> "Thought this might be useful for you."
> [Optional: soft CTA — "If you'd like to talk through how this applies to your situation, I'm happy to jump on a call."]

---

### Pattern 3: The Social Proof Lead
Open with a result, then explain how.

**Structure:**
> "[Client/customer] went from [before] to [after] in [timeframe]."
> [Brief story of how]
> [What it means for the reader]
> [CTA]

---

### Pattern 4: The Direct Re-engagement
For cold or lapsed prospects. Honest and brief.

**Structure:**
> "It's been a while since we connected."
> [One line reminder of who you are / what you do]
> [One honest line: "Things may have changed for you — or they may not have."]
> [Soft ask: "Worth a quick catch-up?" / "Still relevant?"]

---

### Pattern 5: The Announcement Done Right
For product launches, new offerings, events.

**Structure:**
> [The news — one sentence]
> [Why it matters to them specifically]
> [What they can do now]
> [CTA]

**Key rule:** The announcement is never the headline — *what it means for the reader* is the headline.

## Subject Line Formulas That Work in B2B

### Curiosity-Based
- "The reason [common outcome] usually fails"
- "What we learned from [X] clients in [industry]"
- "Why [common belief] is actually backwards"

### Specificity-Based
- "[Specific result] in [timeframe] — here's how"
- "3 things [job title]s do differently"
- "The [industry] benchmark that should concern you"

### Direct / Plain
- "Quick question, [First Name]"
- "Following up on [topic]"
- "Are you still working on [pain point]?"
- "[Client name] + [Your company] — next step"

## What Kills B2B Email

Avoid these patterns:

| ❌ Pattern | ✅ Better Alternative |
|-----------|----------------------|
| "Hope this email finds you well" | Start with the point |
| "I wanted to reach out because..." | Just say the thing |
| "As per my last email..." | Restate context briefly, move on |
| "I'd love to pick your brain" | Make a specific ask |
| "We're excited to announce..." | Lead with what it means to them |
| Multiple CTAs in one email | One CTA only |
| Long paragraphs | 2-3 sentences max per paragraph |
| Generic subject lines ("Following up") | Specific and relevant |

## B2B Email Benchmarks (Reference)

Use these when analyzing client email performance:

| Metric | B2B Average | Good | Excellent |
|--------|-------------|------|-----------|
| Open Rate | 20-25% | 25-35% | 35%+ |
| Click-Through Rate | 2-3% | 3-5% | 5%+ |
| Reply Rate (outreach) | 1-5% | 5-10% | 10%+ |
| Unsubscribe Rate | <0.5% | <0.3% | <0.1% |

*Sources: Mailchimp, HubSpot, Salesforce B2B benchmarks. Adjust expectations based on list quality and audience segment.*

## Tone Notes for 5 Cypress Client Types

**B2B Tech clients:**
- Buyers are often technical + business decision-makers
- Value specificity, ROI clarity, and proof
- Skeptical of hype — ground claims in data
- Appreciate efficiency: get to the point fast

**Medical/Health clients:**
- Buyers include clinical staff and administrators
- Value compliance, outcomes, and workflow impact
- Risk-averse — proof and peer validation matter
- Avoid overpromising; be measured and precise
